Uniaxial Fatigue

Select the info icon or drop down caret to the right of each model for more info!

Plasticity Correction

For uniaxial or proportional loading, Neuber’s rule can be used to obtain the elastic-plastic stress/strain history from the purely elastic stress/strain history. The Ramberg-Osgood constitutive relation is used to obtain the cyclic stress-strain curve up to the first turning point (or reversal). The Masing’s hysteresis relation is then used for the remaining loading history, using the most recent reversal point to capture the effects of cycle closure and material memory.


Cycle Counting

There are many variations of uniaxial cycle counting algorithms, and we choose the most popular one "Downing-Socie Algorithm I (DS1)". The DS1 algorithm requires that the complete loading history be known prior to cycle counting, such that the data can be initially reordered and filtered within the algorithm. The DS1 method is used for a Level 2 Method C assessment in API 579-1, Part 14, Paragraph 14.4.3.4.2. Each cycle physically corresponds to a closed loop in the stress/strain hysteresis curve.

The elastic/plastic loading history is required for uniaxial fatigue damage assessments, however, any history or response may be cycle counted alone.

Fatigue Damage

The fatigue damage assessment procedures are strain-life approaches that are presented in Draper's textbook and are included in the most recent version of API 579-1. The three included strain-life models are uniaxial strain-life without mean stress, uniaxial strain-life with mean stress, and Smith-Watson-Topper.


Multiaxial Fatigue

Select the info icon or drop down caret to the right of each model for more info!

Plasticity Correction

For multiaxial states of stress (either proportional or non-proportional), the incremental Neuber’s rule, along with an appropriate elastic-plastic constitutive relation, can be used to estimate the desired elastic-plastic stress and strain components from the purely elastic stress and strain components.

The elastic values are typically obtained from a linear elastic Finite Element Analysis (FEA). Any constitutive model may be used, but accuracy is key. Some account for isotropic or kinematic hardening alone, while others account for both.

Cycle Counting

The most well established methods for multiaxial fatigue are critical plane methods. These methods use uniaxial cycle counting algorithms, and case specific damage models, but search all planes of interest to determine the maximum plane of damage. The time consuming component of these methods is not in extensive computations, but in iterating the procedure a large number of times. While popular, these methods can only capture damage occurring on a single plane in a fixed direction. Alternatively, when damage is known to occur on multiple planes, cycle counting methods that count all components of loading are recommended. These methods (e.g. Wang-Brown and PDMR) apply the uniaxial theories of counting physical hysteresis curves to counting the equivalent stress or strain of a multiaxial loading history (where there is no unified physical interpretation of a cycle). Both methods have their individual nuances; however, we have included the Wang-Brown Cycle Counting method.

The entire elastic/plastic loading tensor is input into the cycle counting methods. A counting method currently requires the selection of a damage method (one may not be selected without the other).

Fatigue Damage

The strain-life damage models that are considered include the Brown-Miller, Fatemi-Socie, and Smith-Watson-Topper models. The crack that is predicted by the Brown-Miller damage model is assumed to initiate in Mode II. The Brown-Miller model also requires an additional auxiliary parameter to account for mean stress effects. As an attempt to improve the Brown-Miller damage model for shear plane dominant cracks, Fatemi-Socie proposed that the normal stress (rather than the normal strain) on the plane of maximum shear be considered. Neither of the above two models were developed for tensile (Mode I) cracks, which is the intention of the Smith, Watson, and Topper (SWT) model.
